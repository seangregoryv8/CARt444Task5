philosophy.pla

why.am

Most of what I do centers around a set of hardened principles and guidelines, taught to me by my past of computers, alongside my upbringing.

I grew up in a blue-collar family, where the value of hard work and practicality was instilled in me from a young age. My father, a skilled carpenter, taught me the importance of understanding how things work and fixing them when they break. This hands-on approach to problem-solving has greatly influenced my design philosophy, which emphasizes functionality and clarity over aesthetics.

inside_the_box.break

Inside the box, I operate within a set of constraints that define both the form and function of my work. These boundaries are not limitations but rather the foundation upon which I build.

I believe that constraint is not the enemy of creativity, but rather its catalyst. By working within defined parameters, I can focus on innovation and problem-solving, pushing the boundaries of what is possible while maintaining a clear sense of purpose and direction.

When one is told to think outside the box, it may impose users with a sense of freedom of expression, with unlimited possibilities, to not ever hold back.

In reality, I believe that thinking outside this theoretical box encourages a loss of vision, focus, and overall creativity, as it avoids the difficult roads and paths that make better art.

statement.iam

What can systems of old teach us? What do we know about these old systems? And what do we not?

I work in a hands-on approach, pulling from analog media, physical carpentry, and various different engines to create environments that behave on their own terms. My works ask important questions, what these old systems would look like should they gather a mind of their own.

I’m drawn to cosmic horror, not as imagery, but as structure. The idea of an entity running beneath our very existence, following and obeying rules we don’t understand. Rather than explaining, I build up these rules and let them breathe on their own, giving the user the opportunity to see systems loop, degrade, respond incorrectly, or evolve in ways that feel slightly off. I set up rules so you can understand them and see when they’re broken, understanding when something feels slightly off. I want you to understand a system enough to see it doing something just outside of your control.

My process is closer to fabrication than traditional art-making. I treat code, assets, characters, animations, and set pieces like components in a machine, assembling and reworking them until they start to push back. When one notices visual glitches or instability within the machine, they are not problems that need fixing, they’re a sign of a solid foundation generating natural error to keep you on your toes.

I’m not interested in static pieces. I want to build things that feel alive, persistent, and, most important, hostile. Just like the analog systems of old, they run in the background without you knowing, with or without your input, doing things you may not expect, and hiding secrets you’ll never know.
